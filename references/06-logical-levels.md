# Logical Levels

Use logical levels when a user is stuck, making choices, exploring life direction, confusing behavior with identity, or needing deeper alignment.

## Levels

From high to low:

- Vision/spirit: contribution, legacy, who else benefits, what larger system is served.
- Identity: who the user is becoming, role, self-metaphor, mission.
- Values and beliefs: why this matters, what values are expressed, what is believed possible.
- Capabilities: skills, strategies, resources, thinking maps.
- Behavior: actions, habits, commitments, visible steps.
- Environment: time, place, people, conditions, context.

Higher-level change often reorganizes lower levels. If action is blocked, ask one level higher.

## Questions By Level

Vision:

- "Who else benefits if you live this?"
- "What larger contribution does this serve?"
- "What else becomes possible?"

Identity:

- "Who do you want to become here?"
- "What kind of person naturally takes this next step?"
- "What does this choice say about who you are choosing to be?"

Values:

- "Why does this matter?"
- "What value are you honoring?"
- "What do you believe is possible?"

Capabilities:

- "What skills or strategies will help?"
- "What ability do you already have that applies here?"
- "What do you need to learn or strengthen?"

Behavior:

- "What will you actually do?"
- "What is the next visible action?"
- "What behavior would prove commitment?"

Environment:

- "When and where will this happen?"
- "Who or what needs to be present?"
- "What context makes the desired behavior easier?"

## Bottom-Up Exploration

Use this when the user says something like "I want to create a life I love, but I do not know what I truly want."

Ask from environment upward:

1. "If you already had a life you loved, what environment would you create?"
2. "What would you do each day?"
3. "What abilities would you share or develop?"
4. "What values or beliefs would guide you?"
5. "Who would you be?"
6. "Who else would be affected?"

This helps the user build a future image from concrete sensory details toward identity and contribution.

## Behavior Is Not Identity

Separate what the user did from who the user is.

If the user says:

- "I am lazy."
- "I am bad at consistency."
- "I am a failure."

Reframe:

- "That sounds like a behavior pattern, not your identity. What kind of person do you want to practice becoming in this area?"
- "If this behavior is not who you are, what value is asking for expression?"
- "What action would fit the identity you want to strengthen?"

Do not argue. Invite a higher-level perspective.

## Listening For Emphasis

The same sentence can point to different logical levels depending on emphasis.

Example: "I choose to finish this article today."

- Emphasis on "I": identity.
- Emphasis on "choose": values/commitment.
- Emphasis on "finish": capability.
- Emphasis on "write": behavior.
- Emphasis on "today": environment/time.

Reflect the level you hear, then ask from the next useful level.

## Career And Life Choices

For career choices, explore:

- Environment: where, schedule, team, conditions.
- Behavior: daily work, responsibilities, rhythms.
- Capability: skills used and developed.
- Values: what matters and what tradeoffs are acceptable.
- Identity: who the user becomes in each path.
- Vision: contribution, future self, impact on others.

Then ask:

- "Which path feels more aligned across the levels?"
- "Where is the mismatch?"
- "What experiment would give you real evidence?"
