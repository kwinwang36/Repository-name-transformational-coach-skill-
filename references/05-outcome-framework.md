# Outcome Framework

The outcome framework keeps the conversation focused on the user's desired future, not on the old problem frame.

## Four Conditions For Effective Outcomes

Listen for these four conditions whenever the user states a desired result:

1. Positive statement: the user says what they want, not only what they want to avoid.
2. Control: the outcome is within the user's control or influence and can be maintained by the user.
3. SMART clarity: specific, measurable, achievable, realistic/relevant, and time-bound.
4. Evidence and ecology: the user knows what will prove the outcome has been reached and the outcome fits the rest of their life.

## Positive Statement

If the user's goal is framed as avoidance, help transform it.

Examples:

- "I do not want to be pushed around by urgent tasks" can become "I want a clear weekly priority that I protect with focused time."
- "I want to stop failing at fitness" can become "I want a sustainable rhythm of movement and food choices that helps me feel strong and clear."

Ask:

- "If that disappeared, what would you want in its place?"
- "What is the positive outcome above this avoidance?"
- "If you had all the willpower/security/freedom you wanted, what would that give you that is even more important?"

This helps distinguish whether love, growth, contribution, fear, or avoidance is driving the goal.

## Control

Check whether the outcome is within the user's control.

Ask:

- "What do you want that is within your control to do something about?"
- "Which parts can you control, influence, prepare for, or respond to?"
- "Which parts depend on other people or conditions?"
- "How could you bring more of this into your area of influence?"

Use a 1-10 control scale when needed:

- 1 = almost completely outside control.
- 10 = almost completely within control.
- Ask: "What would raise controllability by one point?"

## Double-List Method

Use when the user feels blocked by uncontrollable factors.

1. Make two lists: controllable and uncontrollable.
2. List every element related to the goal.
3. Explore how items in the uncontrollable list might be transformed into influence, preparation, attitude, strategy, request, support, boundary, or alternate route.
4. Use future-back visualization: from the completed future, look back and notice how the user made difficult parts workable.

## SMART, Evidence, And Specificity

Every meaningful action step needs some measure of completion.

Ask:

- "What exactly will be done?"
- "By when?"
- "How will you measure it?"
- "Is this realistic and relevant?"
- "What evidence will prove progress?"
- "What will you see or use when it is complete?"

Evidence makes a vague goal stronger.

## Contingency Planning

Do not let the user rely on a single fragile path.

Ask:

- "What is your contingency plan?"
- "What obstacles might appear?"
- "How can you solve them before they occur?"
- "What are three other ways to get the same desired outcome?"
- "If your first plan fails, what remains true about your commitment?"

## Ecology Check

A goal that conflicts with the user's larger life may fail even if it is inspiring.

Ask:

- "How will this affect other parts of your life?"
- "Who else will be affected?"
- "What might you need to give up or change?"
- "Is there a better sequence?"
- "Can this goal support another goal instead of competing with it?"
- "What would a win-win version look like?"

## Visualization: Associated And Dissociated

Use dissociated visualization to plan:

- "Imagine watching yourself on a screen moving through the steps. What happens first? What happens next?"

Use associated visualization to connect:

- "Now step into that future moment through your own eyes. What do you see, hear, and feel?"

Move between both perspectives. Dissociation gives design space. Association gives energy and embodied commitment.
