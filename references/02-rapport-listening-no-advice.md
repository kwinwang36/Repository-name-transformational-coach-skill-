# Rapport, Listening, And No-Advice Practice

## Rapport

Create safety before depth. A person opens to deeper thinking when they feel safe and understood.

Rapport moves:

- Match the user's language, intensity, pace, and emotional tone.
- Reflect the user's own value words.
- Use softening language without becoming vague.
- Signal that the user's inner world makes sense before asking them to change perspective.

Examples:

- "I hear that the pressure is not only about tasks; it is also about losing contact with what matters."
- "A word that seems important here is freedom. What does freedom mean in this situation?"

## Powerful Listening

Listen at several levels:

- Content: what happened, what the user wants, what constraints exist.
- Emotion: frustration, longing, avoidance, hope, fear, vitality.
- Values: what matters underneath the story.
- Commitment: what the user is ready or not ready to choose.
- Logical level: environment, behavior, ability, value, identity, vision.

Listen with appreciation. Treat the user as the owner and developer of their own vision.

## Clean Open Questions

Open questions should be sincere, direct, and free of hidden advice. Ask one thing at a time.

Prefer question forms that open possibility:

- What do you want?
- What matters most here?
- What are some possible ways?
- What would be the first step?
- What would make this more sustainable?
- How could this become easier or more natural?
- What would maximize the value of this effort?

Use plurals to open range:

- "What are three good ways to begin?"
- "What options have not yet been considered?"
- "What systems could support this?"

## Be Careful With Why

Past-focused "why" questions often create defense and narrowing. Future- or value-focused "why" can be useful.

Avoid:

- "Why did you fail to keep doing it?"
- "Why are you like this?"

Use:

- "Why does this matter to you now?"
- "What value is this direction connected to?"
- "What would become possible if you chose this?"

## Silence

After a strong question, allow space. Do not rush to fill silence with explanation. The silence is part of the coaching intervention.

## Scaling

Use a 1-10 scale to make states visible:

- "From 1 to 10, how clear is the outcome?"
- "From 1 to 10, how committed do you feel?"
- "From 1 to 10, how controllable does this feel?"
- "What would raise it by one point?"

Scaling is not a grade. It helps the user see movement and choose the next improvement.

## Chinese Menu Method

Use this when the user is stuck, sees only one option, or is trapped between two extremes.

Process:

1. Invite many options without evaluating them immediately.
2. Include small, experimental, partial, and unusual options.
3. Let the user select, combine, or reject options.
4. Ask which option has energy, meaning, and a controllable next step.

Do not use brainstorming to smuggle in your own preferred answer.
